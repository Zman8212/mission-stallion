Weighted Sleep Debt (h/night) — Last 14 Days
What this is. A flat 7-day average has a cliff: Jul 7's 4.5h night counts at full weight today and then vanishes completely tomorrow, so the number improves on its own without you doing anything. This metric fixes that. Every night counts a little less the further back it is, fading smoothly instead of dropping off a ledge: last night 100%, three nights ago 75%, a week ago 37%, two weeks ago 14%. A night's weight roughly halves every five days.
Why 14 days and not 7 or 30. Van Dongen 2003 restricted people for 14 straight nights and the damage was still accumulating linearly at day 14: no plateau, no adaptation. Worse, subjective sleepiness saturated early while performance kept falling, which means chronic debt is something you cannot feel and have to compute. A flat 7-day window hard-zeroes 37% of the history that still matters. A 30-day window adds about 2% and has no experimental support at all, because nobody has ever run the study past 14 days. The 7-day fade constant is not invented: it is the decay fitted to the lab data in the Unified Model of Performance, validated across five studies from four labs.
The read right now: the pay-down stalled, the debt ticked back up to +0.91. Jul 16's 9.1h bought the fortnight low (+0.65h); since then the week has run 5.2, 7.1, 7.5, 6.9, four straight nights under target, and the line has drifted back up through +0.94 to +0.91. The fortnight still brackets between the +1.48h Jul 7 peak, now 39% paid down, and the Jul 16 low. The two flat averages tell the same story, 14-day 7.22h and 7-day 7.03h: the deficit is chronic, roughly 47-58 minutes a night, not one bad stretch. Variance sits at ±1.18h (swing 5.2h to 9.1h), past the ±1h target. Regularity is still the lever, not duration. Windred 2024 (UK Biobank) found sleep regularity predicted mortality more strongly than sleep duration, so a steady near-midnight onset is worth more than any catch-up night, and costs less than any crash night.

The lab studies that measured real sleep with electrodes, not self-report, converge on ~8h: Van Dongen 2003 modelled sleep need at 8.16h, Belenky's 9h-in-bed group achieved 7.93h, and Rupp 2009 gave people 10 hours in bed at home and they still only slept 7.99h. That last one is the ceiling: you cannot brute-force past about 8h of actual sleep, and trying tanks your efficiency. Note also what the athlete literature actually says. The famous Stanford "athletes need 10 hours" study (Mah 2011) used 10h as the time-in-bed goal; the players' measured sleep went 6.7h to 8.5h. Nobody in that study slept 10 hours. At Zak's 90% efficiency, 8.0h asleep needs 8h53m in bed, so a 9:00am wake means lights out by midnight. That is the single number this whole page now hangs on.
