Weighted Sleep Debt (h/night) — Last 14 Days
What this is. A flat 7-day average has a cliff: Jul 7's 4.5h night counts at full weight today and then vanishes completely tomorrow, so the number improves on its own without you doing anything. This metric fixes that. Every night counts a little less the further back it is, fading smoothly instead of dropping off a ledge: last night 100%, three nights ago 75%, a week ago 37%, two weeks ago 14%. A night's weight roughly halves every five days.
Why 14 days and not 7 or 30. Van Dongen 2003 restricted people for 14 straight nights and the damage was still accumulating linearly at day 14: no plateau, no adaptation. Worse, subjective sleepiness saturated early while performance kept falling, which means chronic debt is something you cannot feel and have to compute. A flat 7-day window hard-zeroes 37% of the history that still matters. A 30-day window adds about 2% and has no experimental support at all, because nobody has ever run the study past 14 days. The 7-day fade constant is not invented: it is the decay fitted to the lab data in the Unified Model of Performance, validated across five studies from four labs.
The read right now: five straight nights under target, and the debt is back to +0.98, its highest since Jul 10. Jul 16's 9.1h bought the fortnight low (+0.65h); every night since has run short, 5.2, 7.1, 7.5, 6.9, 6.6, and the line has climbed back through +0.88 and +0.91 to +0.98. That erases the entire gain the 9.1h night bought. The Jul 7 spike has now aged out of the fortnight, so the visible peak is Jul 9's +1.27h, which flatters the chart: the debt is not far off where the window starts. The two flat averages tell the same story, 14-day 7.37h and 7-day 7.00h: the deficit is chronic, roughly 38-60 minutes a night, not one bad stretch. Variance sits at ±1.19h (swing 5.2h to 9.1h), past the ±1h target and slightly worse than last week. Regularity is still the lever, not duration. Windred 2024 (UK Biobank) found sleep regularity predicted mortality more strongly than sleep duration, so a steady near-midnight onset is worth more than any catch-up night, and costs less than any crash night. Last night is the proof and the warning at once: the onset was finally good at 12:31am, but an 8:16am wake cut it to 6.6h, so the earliest bedtime of the week still produced a below-target night. Onset and wake have to hold together.

The lab studies that measured real sleep with electrodes, not self-report, converge on ~8h: Van Dongen 2003 modelled sleep need at 8.16h, Belenky's 9h-in-bed group achieved 7.93h, and Rupp 2009 gave people 10 hours in bed at home and they still only slept 7.99h.
